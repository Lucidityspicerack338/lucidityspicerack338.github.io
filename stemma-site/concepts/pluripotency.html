Pluripotency

One cell. Every possible fate. Scroll to watch a single stem cell decide what it will become — and how scientists learned to talk it back.

The starting pointA pluripotent stem cell sits at the top of the landscape — genetically capable of becoming almost any cell type in the body.
Commitment beginsAs the cell receives signals, it rolls into a valley. Each valley is a lineage — a narrowing set of options.
The ridges lock inOnce past a ridge, the cell can't easily cross back. Its gene expression pattern has committed to a path.
A specialised cellBy the valley floor, the cell has become something specific — a neuron, a muscle fibre, a blood cell — and stopped being anything else.

This picture has a name

Biologists call it Waddington's landscape — a metaphor from 1957 that still guides how we think about differentiation. Height represents developmental potential. Losing height means losing options. For decades, the road was thought to run only one way.

Then the road reversed

In 2006, Shinya Yamanaka showed that four transcription factors, switched on in an already-specialised adult cell, could push it back up the landscape — undoing commitment and restoring pluripotency.

OCT4SOX2KLF4MYC

This reprogrammed cell is called an induced pluripotent stem cell — an iPSC. It behaves like it never differentiated at all.
