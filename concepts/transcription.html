CONCEPT 02 · MOLECULAR GENETICS

Transcription

"Reading the master template."

How does the cell open the secure vaults of DNA to copy its instructions? Scroll to watch RNA polymerase bind, slide, and assemble a temporary mRNA messenger molecule.

METAPHOR: POLYMERASE ALIGNMENT
Stage I · Promoter Recognition

Initiation Cascade

The process begins when RNA Polymerase scans the DNA sequence, seeking a distinct sequence known as a promoter. Once located, the enzyme latches onto the double-stranded DNA template.

Stage II · Unwinding the Helix

The Unzipping Bubble

To read the code, the polymerase must physically separate the hydrogen-bonded base pairs. It uncoils a short segment of the DNA, creating a localized transcription bubble.

Stage III · Polymerization

Elongation & Assembly

Using one DNA strand as a template, the polymerase threads matching ribonucleotides from the nucleoplasm, forging covalent bonds to form a single, complementary messenger RNA (mRNA) strand.

Stage IV · Transcript Release

Termination

Upon reaching a genetic terminator signal, the polymerase complex releases both the finished mRNA transcript and the double-helix, which instantly zips back together.

THE MOLECULAR MACHINERY

RNA Polymerase II

RNA Polymerase II is the primary molecular copying machine in eukaryotic cells. It handles the critical duty of synthesizing messenger RNAs (mRNAs) from protein-coding genes. Acting as a master architect, it unzips DNA, stabilizes loose nucleotides, verifies complementary pairings, and links them at speeds averaging 40-50 nucleotides per second.

This process of transcription is the first absolute prerequisite of life. Without this sequence copy, genetic codes would remain permanently locked inside the nuclear vault, unable to interact with the ribosome to build proteins.
